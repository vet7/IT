Project Idea

Project Idea


Overview

Imagine an instant food delivery system applied to online shopping. My project idea is to create a mobile application to support a service that streamlines the delivery of online purchases directly to consumers. As an online shopper, you will be able to purchase items, such as clothes, stationery, bathroom essentials and other easy-to-package products, from select stores around your area and someone will be able to deliver this to you within the hour.

Motivation

While many of us already use food and liquor delivery services, there is a shortage of courier services offering the same for online shopping. Even in terms of existing courier services, these usually take at least three hours to deliver. It appears that whilst online shopping trends have taken off, delivery services are still in need of catching up.

Statista recently published results from their survey:

‘As internet access and adoption are rapidly increasing worldwide, the number of digital buyers keeps climbing every year. In 2019, an estimated 1.92 billion people purchased goods or services online, and during the same year, e-retail sales surpassed 3.5 trillion U.S. dollars worldwide’ (Coppola 2021, para. 1).

Similarly, the Australian context is no exception:

‘We are in an interesting position within Australia. Ecommerce orders were on the rise due to safety concerns of shopping instore and lockdowns issued across Australian states at the start of 2020…and despite this demand, delivery networks are struggling to keep up’ (Comestri 2021, p. 6).

As the proposed idea will have humble beginnings, it will only be able to address the efficiency in the surrounding area of the consumer. However, it is nevertheless a start, and will contribute towards satisfying online-purchasing demands, supporting local businesses, and addressing safety concerns especially given our current context.

Description

Streamlined:

This app would simply give a new dimension to B2C services and mobile apps. It will be a one-stop-shop for a multitude of products, so long as they can be safely transported by a courier. It will increase efficiency, support local businesses and jobs, whilst allowing consumers to contribute to the economy from the convenience of their living room.

Simple:

A user-friendly interface that makes it easy for users to scroll through a selection of stores in their area, to then select from a variety of products from that store, combined with a clear check-out process that takes secure online transactions.

The app will also need to be designed in a way that is simple for businesses to use. A logistical challenge here is that the app will need to address the amount of data that will have to be stored due to the large number of items that shops may want to include. This can partly be solved by adding a feature that caps the number of items that each store can list. Through a subscriber model, each business can choose data packages based on different price levels.

Due to the familiarity with similar delivery applications, most users will already be somewhat adept at most of the features and processes. The app will be structured around maps and filters which will allow the user to select stores based on what products or services they are interested in, within a given radius. However, that is not to say it can’t also be unique!

Smart:

Real-time geolocation tracking of the order. This feature has been widely used by food delivery services and essentially allows the consumer to track the product in real time, by visually identifying on the map where the courier is at any given time. This allows both the consumer and the business to plan their time and resources.

The idea would be that the drivers be ready in strategic locations to allow them to pick up the order instantly and deliver it. An efficient notification system will need to be implemented to allow for both the couriers and the stores to prepare and deliver the products as quickly as possible.

Safe:

Safety has been a key topic since the start of the pandemic and has taken new central role in the dynamics of urban societies. City and shopping centres are empty because consumers are scared of cramping within restricted spaces. The contactless delivery and instant delivery of products allows for the transaction of goods and service to continue within a safe space, supporting both businesses and consumers.

It is impossible to separate consumerism and capitalism from the inevitable effect it has on the environment. This app seeks to have a positive effect on the environment by decreasing the amount of travelling that consumers would have to undertake to shop for products.

Support:

Business and customer support either listed somewhere easy to find or integrated into the design.

Tools & Technologies

In order to create the app and design the basic interface, the programming software required to do this will depend on the language we choose to adopt. For example, the most often used language for designing an app for android devices is Java (BuildFire n.d.a), which we would then use Eclipse IDE to develop and edit our code. Additionally, there are programs specifically designed for building apps such as Android Studio for android (BuildFire n.d.a).

We will also need do some unit testing called Test-Driven Development, to test and debug the program as we go. Some options include PyTest for Python, Quick for iOS, or Karma for Javascript (Codementor 2015).

Perhaps the most challenging part of this project is the integration of additional features or functions, depending on how many we wish to include. A particularly important feature is that the app relies heavily on tracking a geolocation for a device. For geolocation, there are two main ways to integrate this: (1) Internet IP address (2) GPS device; the second option is device dependent and requires a lot more work than the first (Dwyer 2019). For this reason, and to make things simpler, programming in Python could be a solid option as we can more easily learn the language and link IP address data (IPinfo n.d.).

Furthermore, we may need to have a thorough understanding of data storage so that there might be an option for tracking and updating stock levels for any given online store. We will need to look into creating a cloud-based server as well as the associated back-end processes (Buildfire n.d.b).

Skills Required

Essentially, the feasibility of this project will depend on how complex we want the app to be and how dedicated the team is to the idea, acknowleding the fact that a lot of work will be required from each of us to accomplish and submit the final product. That being said, if everyone is up for it, based on the skills we currently have the project is indeed ambitious but doable within the given timeframe if we plan strategically, distribute the workload, and structure our learning and programming accordingly (BuildFire n.d.a). We will need to: (1) list down the core functions, (2) do a rough sketch of the app’s user interface, (3) plan the UI flow, (4) design the backend - the database, (5) then design the front-end otherwise called UX wireframes (Codementor 2015).

Given that it is A LOT of work for an introductory course, and that most of the team also have full-time jobs, another option is to scale the project down to a more manageable size, and to simply create a prototype that we may then wish to further develop at a later stage.

Outcome

Traditional online shopping can be somewhat of a dull experience. When buying online you can often feel detached from the item until you receive it which can often take days or weeks. Through this app, the real-time nature of the shopping experience will allow consumers to form an immediate bond with the product as it makes its way (on the map) to their homes. If the project is successful, it will present an efficient solution to the influx of deliverables, as well as reimagine and improve the overall experience of online shopping.
